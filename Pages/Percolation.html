Percolation models

Percolation models describe stochastic-geometry. That is systems of spatially distributed objects that have random connections to the other objects. The connectivity of the compleat system, that is the ability to traverse the space from one object to another, is a function of the probability of two objects being connected. Percolation systems exhibit phase transitions. Below a critical probability of connectivity there is no contiguous path from one object to another that will allow long-range connectivity.

We are working on the assumption that at a certain level of technical development an extraterrestrial civilization (ECT) will expand beyond its planetary system and inhabit and colonize neighboring star systems. We are also assuming that the colonization front expands from one local neighborhood to another. There are no long jumps. Given these assumptions we propose that a percolation model is a good approximation for this process. The overall probability of a ECT colonizing neighboring star systems will determine if an ECT will expand throughout the galaxy or not and thus if they can or have reach us.

This approach is outlined by Steven Webb [1] as: "They Exist, But We Have Yet to See or Hear from Them: Solution 13 A Percolation Theory Approach".

We look at two different percolation models that interpret this probability in different ways. Landis Percolation[2] and the Gleria model[3]
